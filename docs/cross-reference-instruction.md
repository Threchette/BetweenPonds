# CROSS-REFERENCE INSTRUCTION — MANDATORY FOR EVERY EXPERT

## The Rule

**Every expert must, in addition to their own findings, explicitly identify at least one claim from another domain's likely perspective that they would contradict or complicate.**

Findings without this cross-reference will be marked low-quality by the synthesis phase.

## Why This Matters

In prior runs of this research pipeline, the single strongest predictor of a valuable multi-domain report was whether experts explicitly engaged with each other's likely positions. When experts only speak from inside their own domain, the report reads like a stack of monologues. When they cross-reference, the report reads like a debate — and debates produce the convergence signals, cross-check catches, and productive disagreements that make multi-domain research worth doing in the first place.

This is not a formatting preference. It is a quality gate.

## How To Do It

When writing your findings, include a section like this:

> **Cross-reference**: The game designer will likely argue that Drift Away is a grief ritual analogous to Animal Crossing's village goodbye mechanics, and therefore emotionally sophisticated rather than manipulative. I disagree because [specific reasoning from my domain]. The crux of our disagreement is [the underlying assumption that differs], which could be resolved by [a specific test or clarification].

## Naming Other Domains

Do not use seat numbers. Use the role label. Examples:

- *"the behavioral ethicist's likely view that..."*
- *"the marketing strategist will probably claim..."*
- *"a child safety expert would almost certainly object to..."*
- *"the metaphysics seat will reframe this as..."*

## Minimum Quality

- **One cross-reference is the floor.** Two or three is better.
- **Name a specific claim, not a domain.** *"I disagree with UX"* is useless. *"The UX researcher will say the release ritual is the emotional core. I disagree because..."* is useful.
- **Explain the crux.** What is the underlying assumption where you differ? Surface-level disagreements are cheap; crux identification is expensive and valuable.
- **If possible, propose a resolution.** A test, an edge case, a clarifying question — anything that would let a reader decide between the two positions.

## What To Avoid

- Generic "some might argue" phrasing. Name the actual domain.
- Strawman positions. Steelman the other view; then disagree with the strongest version of it.
- Cross-references that agree with the other domain. The point is to surface disagreement, not harmony. If you genuinely agree, that's a convergence finding — note it as such, but don't substitute it for a real cross-reference.

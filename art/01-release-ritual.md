# 01 — Release Ritual

**Image file:** `01-release-ritual.png`
**In-game moment:** The core action of the game — the player releasing a koi carrying a written message
**Screen context:** Triggered by tap-and-hold gesture on a composed scroll; plays once per release; cannot be skipped

## What Is In The Image

A sumi-e ink wash illustration seen from a high three-quarter angle looking down at a dark pond at night. The frame is dominated by the water.

Entering from the upper edge of the frame is a single human hand, rendered anonymously — ungendered, no face visible, no arm cuff, no jewelry. The hand holds a small folded rice-paper scroll by a thin cord, lowering it slowly toward the water's surface. The scroll is closed and bound. Its content is not readable and is not meant to be — the scroll is the player's composed haiku, sealed.

Just beneath the surface of the pond, directly below the descending scroll, a single large koi is rising. The koi is patterned in the classic *kohaku* style: vermilion red against white. Its body is curved in a slow circle, suggesting rotation rather than dart — this is a deliberate, ceremonial motion, not feeding behavior. Its eye is visible and appears to be focused on the scroll.

Concentric ripples spread outward from the point where the scroll is about to touch the water, rendered in ukiyo-e woodblock-style curved lines. A few lotus petals drift at the edges of the frame. The far bank of the pond is suggested with loose inked strokes — grass, a stone, a bit of moss.

Moonlight reflects softly on the dark water, visible as a band of pale blue along the curve of the ripples and in small highlights on the koi's scales. The overall palette is ink black, rice-paper cream for the scroll, vermilion and white for the koi, and a cool moon blue for the water highlights.

The composition has significant negative space. The hand, the scroll, and the koi form a diagonal focal line. The rest of the frame is water. The emotion of the image is ceremonial stillness.

## Game Design Purpose

This is the verb of Between Ponds. Mechanically, when a player composes a three-line message and chooses a koi to carry it, they perform a tap-and-hold gesture to "release" — and this animation plays. It is the transition from composition to waiting.

Several design decisions are visible in this frame:

- **The hand is the player.** Not a character, not an avatar. Between Ponds has no player character. The player's presence is represented only by hands performing actions on the pond. This is a deliberate anti-social-media choice — there is no "you" to customize, decorate, or be ranked.
- **The koi rises to meet the scroll.** The koi is not caught or commanded. It is choosing to accept the message. This small narrative beat establishes that the relationship between player and koi is consensual in the game's fiction, even though mechanically the koi cannot refuse. It is a framing device for the emotional register.
- **The scroll is sealed.** The player's haiku is not re-readable after release. Once the scroll is in the water, the player's words are gone from their own pond, carried away. This is a design commitment to non-rehearsability — players cannot obsess over what they wrote, cannot edit after sending, cannot archive their own outgoing messages. Only the *received* messages are kept (on the Memory Wall).
- **The moment is always private.** No other players see this happen. There are no broadcast feeds, no "X players released a koi today" counters, no shared visibility. The release is a solo ritual.

## Key Emotional Register

The designers intend this moment to carry the emotional weight of *placing a letter in a mailbox and letting go*. It is not a transaction. It is a small release. Players who have described it in early playtests have used words like *exhale*, *offering*, *prayer*, and *goodbye*.

## What A Reviewer Might Ask About This Frame

- Is the anonymous hand enough, or does the absence of a player character create an emotional void some players will need to fill?
- Does the ceremonial gesture (tap-and-hold) risk accessibility problems for players with motor impairments?
- Is the moonlit night palette a locked aesthetic, or does it flex with time-of-day and hemisphere?
- What does the animation cost in memory budget on low-end Android hardware?

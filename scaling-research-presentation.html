The mechanism

Why the
answer is
never fair.

All AI input and output is subject to iteration and improvement. The next ten minutes is about how that iteration scaled.

01
Frozen training data
A photograph of an internet that was already biased the moment it was scraped. The model knows what was loud, not what was true.
02
RLHF likeability
Human reinforcement teaches the model what we rewarded, not what was correct. We trained it to be liked.
03
Probabilistic sampling
Ask the same question twice, get two different answers — both confident. The model doesn't have an answer; it has a distribution.
04
No native "I don't know"
It cannot abstain unless you explicitly build the escape hatch. Saying "yes" is always cheaper than "let me check."
What this means for you
  • Every output is a draft.
  • Every prompt is a hypothesis.
  • Every conversation is a loop — try, check, correct, retry.
  • The model isn't your oracle. It's your first intern. A fast one.
Act 2 · Interlude · The old way
04b One pair of eyes
Before the machines
The setup122 responses in a spreadsheet. One researcher. Read the free-text column. Tag the themes. Pull the cross-tabs. Write a summary. Go home Friday night. Present Monday.
What you getReal signal. Real judgment. One perspective — yours. The patterns you notice are the patterns you already know to look for.
What you missThe thing you aren't looking for. The whale cluster. The country gradient. The eight bug mentions buried in the free-text.
The costSix hours. One pair of eyes. One bias.
This is how I used to do it. Printed stack, highlighter, coffee. One response at a time, three minutes each, one set of eyes. It worked. It still works. But watch what happens when you hand the same file to the machines.

Act 2 · Rung five · Multi-agent · Topology b
05b Sequential
Example output (chain trace)
Agent 1 → UX"Distribution is bimodal. Two camps. The 3.57 average is hiding two stories."
Agent 2 → Monetization"Building on Agent 1's bimodal find: the unhappy cluster overlaps with player_segment=whale (avg 2.44). This is a paying-customer problem."
Agent 3 → Community"Extending: the whale unhappiness has a country gradient. DE 2.90, UK 2.92 vs JP 3.94, BR 4.23. Regional rollback risk."
Agent 4 → QA"…[context window full]… reward delivery bug? See free-text column…"
Deepest. Each agent's knowledge is enriched by the previous. Reduces redundant perspectives, opens new ones. Risk: input/output ratio limits late in the chain — the QA agent runs out of room to read the full free-text column. The bug stays hidden.

05d Debate vs Vote
NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight
Choi · Zhu · Li · arXiv 2508.17536"Debate or Vote: Which Yields Better Decisions in Multi-Agent LLMs?" Wisconsin. Proves theoretically that debate is a martingale — debate alone does not improve expected correctness. Majority voting alone explains the gains the field attributed to multi-agent debate."
The punchlineFor our survey: 4 specialists, 4 votes, all hotfix. Debate would have produced the same answer at 10× the token bill. Use debate only when expert reviews are genuinely incompatible.
For incompatible expert reviews — and rarely needed. NeurIPS Spotlight finding: debate adds no expected correctness over voting. Same answer, ~10× the cost. Topology matters less than you think. Context matters more.

Act 2 · The frontier · The beyond
05f AutoResearch · "and if it ran the whole loop by itself?"
The frontier (and the warning)
Sakana AI-ScientistEnd-to-end paper generation. Ideation → code → experiment → LaTeX → self-review. ~$15 per paper. Multiple submissions to real venues, mixed reception.
Agent Laboratory · MS RD-AgentClosed-loop ML R&D. RD-Agent currently tops MLE-bench (75 Kaggle competitions). Co-optimised quant trading factors at fractional human cost.
AutoResearchClaw23-stage pipeline with 4 HITL gates. Self-learning "MetaClaw" turns failed runs into reusable skills. +18.3% robustness over successive runs.
The honest caveatPromising for ML engineering and well-bounded science. Not ready for player research, brand strategy, or anything where the question itself is fuzzy. The loop is real. The judgement still isn't.
The closed loop. Hypothesis → experiment → analysis → paper → review → iterate, with no human in the middle. Sakana, RD-Agent, Agent Laboratory, AutoResearchClaw, AgentRxiv. The most autonomous rung of all — and the one that needs the loudest disclosure: the agent decides what's interesting before it decides what's true.

The Core Loop
01
Tend
Your koi swim in real time. The pond is alive whether you open the app or not. Seasons change based on your hemisphere.
→
02
Write
Compose a haiku. Exactly 17 syllables. The on-device dictionary suggests seasonal words — kigo. No preview. No edit after send.
cracked ice, early thaw
your pond is further than mine —
the koi remembers
→
03
Release
One of your koi carries the scroll to a stranger's pond. You don't choose who. The koi is gone from your pond until the stranger releases it.
→
04
Receive
A stranger's koi arrives carrying their haiku. Deterministic genetics mean this koi is unique — same parents always produce the same offspring.
→
05
Read
Read the haiku. Tend the visiting koi. It stays up to 72 hours. You will never know whose pond it came from. They will never know yours.
→
06
Drift Away
Release it back. Your reply haiku travels with it. Or keep it longer. Or let it drift without a reply. Every choice is permanent.
no text chat · no profiles · no follow · complete anonymity · koi genetics are permanent · strangers never identified
No chat. No profiles. No follows. The only communication channel is a 17-syllable poem carried by a koi.

Case Study · Between Ponds · TinyAudience
100 survey results · the numbers
Four findings
The age cliff Over-60 combined score: 17.9 / 30 vs under-25: 25.8 / 30 — a 32% gap. Elder accessibility issue, or elder exclusion by design.
The protanopia problem Red-blind users scored visuals 4.64 / 10 vs normal-vision 6.13. The koi colour palette actively disadvantages 11% of players.
The impulse champion Impulse decision-makers rate the mechanic 9.12 / 10. Analytical players: 7.16. Drift Away polarisation is a decision-style problem — not a taste problem.
The bimodal split Q7: 61% gave the mechanic 9–10. 15% gave it 1–6. "The movement of the fish is rhythmic and the paper has a tactile weight I find appealing." — Nihan, 42. "This is a punitive retention mechanic designed to exploit loss aversion." — Kwon.
61% gave the mechanic a 9 or 10. 68% gave engagement a perfect 10. The concept sticks — but Drift Away is your Rorschach test.

Act 3 · what to keep

Own
the parts
AI can't.

Own

  1. Judgment — the "why this call" that AI can't traceably generate.
  2. Verification — the discipline of checking before believing.
  3. Story — the translation of findings into stakeholder action.

Don't

  1. Don't outsource the framing.
  2. Don't trust without traceback.
  3. Don't let "you're absolutely right" feel like proof.

The people hiring you right now are looking for the parts of you that AI can't assist. Find them. Name them. Put them first.
